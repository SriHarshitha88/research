India and global evidence consistently show that higher ambient PM2.5 exposure increases cardiovascular mortality and morbidity, with usable quantitative effect estimates available from multi‑city Indian time‑series, Delhi mortality/speciation analyses, and global meta‑analyses and GBD outputs. The strongest India‑specific effect numbers are currently for all‑cause mortality (with substantial CVD share) and for PM2.5‑attributable IHD and stroke burden, while detailed India‑only CVD‑specific RRs remain sparse. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10089304/)

## Top 5 papers to prioritize

1. Ambient air pollution and daily mortality in ten cities of India (Lancet Planetary Health, 2024) – First India multi‑city causal PM2.5–mortality study with a clear AF (7.2% of deaths) and city‑level exposure–outcome linkage, directly feeding Step 3 burden attribution. [thelancet](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanplh/article/PIIS2542-5196(24)00114-1/fulltext)
2. Global, regional, and national burden of cardiovascular diseases associated with PM2.5 (2025) – Provides disease‑specific IHD and stroke RRs per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 and national‑level burden estimates, highly compatible with EBOD modelling for India. [journal.hep.com](https://journal.hep.com.cn/RCM/EN/10.31083/RCM27056)
3. Long‑Term PM2.5 Exposure and Risks of Ischemic Heart Disease and Cerebrovascular Disease (2020) – High‑quality meta‑analysis of cohort studies giving robust RRs for IHD and stroke mortality and incident stroke per 10 µg/m³, ideal for concentration‑response functions. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7955467/)
4. Long‑term exposure to particulate matter on cardiovascular and respiratory diseases in LMICs (2023) – LMIC‑focused meta‑analysis yields CVD‑specific RRs for PM2.5 morbidity and mortality that better match Indian context than high‑income‑only studies. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10089304/)
5. State of Global Air Asia report (2025) – Regionally focused assessment quantifying PM2.5‑attributable IHD and stroke DALYs and deaths in South Asia, including India, directly usable as a burden baseline for EBOD. [stateofglobalair](https://www.stateofglobalair.org/sites/default/files/documents/2025-01/soga-asia-report.pdf)

## Biggest evidence gaps for India in this cluster

1. **India‑specific long‑term CVD cohorts with PM2.5 exposure.**  
   There are no large, nationally representative Indian cohort studies tracking individual PM2.5 exposure and cardiovascular incidence/mortality; current RRs are extrapolated from high‑income or broader LMIC meta‑analyses. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7955467/)

2. **Cause‑specific CVD outcomes in Indian time‑series.**  
   Available multi‑city India time‑series analyses primarily report all‑cause mortality, not separate IHD, stroke, or heart failure outcomes, limiting direct use for disease‑specific cardiovascular EBOD estimates. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1352231016309281)

3. **Source‑resolved and PM2.5‑component‑specific CVD risk estimates.**  
   While the Delhi speciation study links certain chemical species to mortality, there is little India‑wide work tying ambient PM2.5 from specific sources (traffic, industry, construction/road dust) to cardiovascular endpoints, which constrains source‑level policy attribution for CVD burden. [stateofglobalair](https://www.stateofglobalair.org/sites/default/files/documents/2025-01/soga-asia-report.pdf)
