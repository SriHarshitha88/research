Heat stress, climate disasters, and livelihood loss are increasingly linked to mental health problems in India, with emerging evidence for anxiety, depression, climate distress, and post‑disaster syndromes such as PTSD, often mediated by heat, displacement, and economic stress.  Below is a consolidated evidence table (14 items) tailored to your cluster, then the top 5 to prioritize and India‑specific evidence gaps. ## Top 5 papers to prioritize first (1‑line reason each)

1. **Mental health after India’s glacial floods (2025)** – India‑specific, quantitative estimate that ~70% of survivors experience significant psychological stress (anxiety, depression, PTSD signals), directly usable as an AF‑like anchor for post‑disaster mental‑health risk. [thinkglobalhealth](https://www.thinkglobalhealth.org/article/mental-health-after-indias-glacial-floods)
2. **Climate change and mental health in India: a narrative review (2025)** – India‑focused synthesis of heat, drought, floods, displacement, and livelihood‑linked anxiety/depression; organizes key RRs and mechanisms for EBOD conceptualization and data‑gaps checks. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12698474/)
3. **The impact of climate change on mental health (2020)** – Global meta‑review giving pooled ORs/RRs for climate‑linked anxiety, depression, and PTSD; provides generic RRs to use in India where local estimates are missing. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2020.00074/full)
4. **WHO Mental health in emergencies fact sheet (2025)** – Global, EBOD‑friendly prevalence estimates (13–22% of disaster‑exposed people with clinically relevant symptoms) that can be used as default priors for India‑specific climate‑related mental‑health modules. [who](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/mental-health-in-emergencies)
5. **Climate‑linked mental health impacts on vulnerable communities (India‑focused, 2025)** – India‑specific, vulnerability‑oriented description of climate‑related anxiety, depression, and PTSD; supports risk‑weighting for farmers, migrants, women, and other EBOD subgroups. [iisppr.org](https://iisppr.org.in/climate-crisis-and-mental-health-impacts-on-vulnerable-communities/)

## 3 biggest evidence gaps for this cluster in India

1. **India‑specific RRs/ORs for climate‑linked mental health outcomes by exposure type and duration.**  
   India‑focused work is largely narrative or cross‑sectional with prevalence percentages; there is a lack of longitudinal or quasi‑experimental studies that provide RRs/HRs for anxiety, depression, PTSD, or climate‑distress per unit of heat, drought duration, or displacement, constraining dose–response modelling in EBOD. [thinkglobalhealth](https://www.thinkglobalhealth.org/article/mental-health-after-indias-glacial-floods)

2. **High‑resolution, subnational estimates of climate‑linked mental‑health burden across states and climate zones.**  
   Most India‑specific evidence comes from single‑disaster case studies (e.g., glacial floods) or qualitative reviews; India‑wide, state‑ or district‑level time‑series linking heat, drought, displacement, and livelihood loss to survey‑ or service‑based mental‑health indicators remain limited. [paramcohort](https://paramcohort.in/feeling-blue-about-climate-change-the-mental-health-impact-in-india-and-why-its-not-just-the-weather/)

3. **Integrated assessment of climate‑stress pathways (heat, air pollution, displacement, livelihood loss) on mental health.**  
   Existing studies typically isolate one exposure (e.g., heat, floods, or displacement); there is little India‑specific work that jointly models how heat stress, air‑pollution‑related stress, climate‑displacement, and chronic livelihood insecurity interact to shape anxiety, depression, and climate‑distress, which limits multi‑exposure EBOD scenarios. [climateconnection.org](https://climateconnection.org.in/updates/eco-anxiety-impact-climate-change-mental-health)
