India‑, South‑Asia, and global evidence link climate variables (temperature, rainfall, relative humidity, and climate‑driven suitability) directly to dengue and malaria transmission, with several India‑specific studies providing quantitative climate‑suitability indices, extended transmission windows, and projected burden increases suitable for EBOD Step 3.  Below is a curated set of 12 key papers/reports. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7490804/)

## Top 5 papers to prioritize first

1. **Dengue situation in India: Suitability and transmission potential under climate change (2020)** – First India‑wide mapping of dengue climate suitability and projected extra months of risk; ideal for defining India‑specific suitability exposure layers. [csir4pi.res](https://csir4pi.res.in/index.php/research/publications/113-dengue-situation-in-india-suitability-and-transmission-potential-model-for-present-and-projected-climate-change-scenarios)
2. **Dengue dynamics, predictions, and future increase under changing climate (2025)** – India‑specific projections of rising dengue incidence and mortality under climate change; directly feeds EBOD counterfactual and scenario burdens. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-85437-w)
3. **Climate change could shift disease burden from malaria to dengue (2020)** – Biophysical model showing how warming elevates dengue suitability and reduces malaria suitability in India; key for balancing dengue vs malaria risk in EBOD. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7490804/)
4. **Distribution expansion of dengue vectors and climate change in South Asia (2022)** – Quantifies projected range expansion of Aedes vectors in India and South Asia; can be used to estimate population newly exposed to dengue‑suitable climates. [agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1029/2021GH000477)
5. **Climate and dengue (Pune, 2025)** – City‑specific climate‑dengue projections with numeric % increase in mortality under 1.5°C, 2°C, and higher warming; highly usable for subnational scenarios and sensitivity bands. [climate.rocksea](https://www.climate.rocksea.org/research/climate-dengue/)

## Biggest evidence gaps for this cluster in India

1. **Quantitative India‑specific RRs/ORs of dengue and malaria incidence per unit change in temperature and rainfall.**  
   Existing work emphasizes suitability indices, extended transmission windows, and projections but rarely provides explicit RRs (e.g., % increase in incidence per 1°C rise or per 100 mm rainfall increment), forcing EBOD to rely on global or mechanistic proxies. [ui.adsabs.harvard](https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024AGUFMGH33B2432B/abstract)

2. **National, high‑resolution spatiotemporal models linking climate variables to actual dengue and malaria case counts.**  
   India has several state‑ or city‑level studies (Delhi, Pune), but there is no operationally calibrated national‑scale time‑series model that links granular temperature, rainfall, and humidity data to weekly/monthly dengue and malaria incidence across all states, which limits EBOD precision. [nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-85437-w)

3. **Integrated assessments of climate–land use–urban form impacts on vector suitability.**  
   Current climate‑suitability studies focus on climate alone; there is limited India‑specific work that jointly quantifies how climate change plus urbanization, land‑use change, and water‑holding structures alter dengue‑vector suitability and malaria risk, which constrains source‑ and policy‑specific attribution. [undp](https://www.undp.org/asia-pacific/blog/climate-change-and-vector-borne-diseases-looming-threat-we-cant-ignore)
