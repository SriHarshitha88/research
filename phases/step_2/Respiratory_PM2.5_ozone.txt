Ambient PM2.5 and ozone are strongly and consistently linked to respiratory morbidity and mortality, with India and South Asia bearing some of the highest exposure and burden levels globally.  Below is an evidence table for 12 high‑quality papers/reports in the **Respiratory–PM2.5/Ozone** cluster, plus a short list of priority items and key Indian evidence gaps. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4740125/)

## Top 5 papers to prioritize first

1. **WHO global air quality guidelines – particulate matter and ozone (2021)** – Provides standardized RRs and AFs per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 and O3, which can be directly applied to India using local exposure data for Step 3. [who](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240034228)
2. **Long‑term exposure to particulate matter and mortality: an update (2024)** – Global meta‑analysis with explicit RRs for PM2.5 and respiratory mortality per 10 µg/m³, ideal for India‑specific burden calculations. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11466858/)
3. **State of Global Air – South Asia regional air quality snapshot (2023)** – Region‑specific PM2.5 and mortality numbers with strong respiratory component; can be disaggregated to India for EBOD burden baselines. [stateofglobalair](https://www.stateofglobalair.org/sites/default/files/documents/2023-09/soga_reg._aq_snapshot_9-11-23_final.pdf)
4. **PM2.5 inhalation exposure and associated health risk in young adults (2026)** – India‑specific ORs/RRs for respiratory symptoms in young adults, useful for morbidity component and youth exposure scenarios. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sustainable-cities/articles/10.3389/frsc.2026.1775496/full)
5. **Long‑term PM2.5 exposure and asthma risk (global cohort, 2023)** – Quantitative RR (21.4% increase per 10 µg/m³) and AF (≈30% of asthma cases) for PM2.5; can be used to apportion India‑level asthma burden to PM2.5. [healthpolicy-watch](https://healthpolicy-watch.news/air-pollution-robs-indian-farmworkers-of-their-health-and-livelihoods/)

## 3 biggest evidence gaps for this cluster in India

1. **India‑specific short‑term and long‑term PM2.5 and O3 RRs for distinct respiratory outcomes**  
   Most India‑focused work is descriptive or review‑style; there is a lack of large India‑specific cohort or time‑series studies that report explicit RRs/HRs for asthma, COPD, pneumonia, and acute respiratory infections per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 or O3, forcing EBOD reliance on global meta‑analyses. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666765724001212)

2. **High‑resolution, city‑ or district‑level exposure–response for PM2.5 and O3 across diverse Indian settings (urban, peri‑urban, rural, industrial, agricultural)**  
   Existing studies often use national‑ or regional‑averaged exposures; India‑wide, spatially explicit models linking gridded PM2.5/O3 to location‑specific respiratory morbidity and mortality are still limited, which constrains subnational EBOD precision. [worldbank](https://www.worldbank.org/en/region/sar/publication/striving-for-clean-air)

3. **Source‑resolved PM2.5 and O3 effects on respiratory health (vehicles, industry, construction dust, road dust, crop burning, ozone interactions)**  
   While India‑specific sources of PM2.5 and O3 are well catalogued, there is limited work that jointly models source contributions and ascribes disease burden (e.g. asthma/COPD cases) to each sector; this limits source‑specific policy‑scenario attribution in EBOD. [sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2667010025001349)
