Flooding is consistently associated with increased diarrhoeal morbidity and outbreaks in India, South Asia, and globally, with several studies and reviews providing relative risks, odds ratios, and excess‑case estimates that can feed an EBOD Step 3 model.  Below is a focused set of 12 key items. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11917333/)

## Top 5 papers to prioritize first

1. **Floods and diarrheal morbidity: Evidence on the relationship, effect modifiers, and attributable risk (J Glob Health, 2022)** – Provides pooled RRs and AFs for flood–diarrhoea associations, directly usable as generic effect sizes in EBOD when India‑specific parameters are missing. [jogh](https://jogh.org/2022/jogh-12-11007/)
2. **Risk factors of diarrhoea among flood victims (Indian J Public Health, 2001)** – India‑specific controlled study comparing flood‑prone vs non‑flood‑prone populations with clear differences in diarrhoea attack rates and identifiable behavioural modifiers. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/epirev/article/27/1/36/520815?login=true)
3. **Exploring the association between floods and diarrhea among children (Disaster Med Public Health Preparedness, 2024)** – Recent analytic study showing 34% higher diarrhoea risk among children exposed to repeated floods, giving a directly usable RR for frequency of flooding. [cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/disaster-medicine-and-public-health-preparedness/article/exploring-the-association-between-floods-and-diarrhea-among-underfive-children-in-rural-india/879B5E8EF1F16D31BA73866E65738E82)
4. **Infectious diarrhea risks as a public health emergency in floods (2024)** – Systematic analysis confirming significant increased diarrhoea risk after floods across pathogens and regions, providing RRs that can be adopted for EBOD sensitivity analysis. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11221827/)
5. **Global health impacts of floods: epidemiologic evidence (Epidemiologic Reviews, 2005)** – Classic global review summarizing pathogen‑specific ORs (e.g. OR 4–5 for paratyphoid and cryptosporidiosis after flooding), useful for bounding high‑risk scenarios and calibrating pathogen‑specific diarrhoeal components. [academic.oup](https://academic.oup.com/epirev/article/27/1/36/520815?login=true)

## Biggest evidence gaps for India in this cluster

1. **Quantitative, India‑wide estimates of diarrhoeal RR and AF specifically attributable to flooding.**  
   Existing Indian evidence is mostly local outbreak investigations and one controlled study; there is no national time‑series or multi‑state panel quantifying diarrhoeal RR per flood event or per day of flooding, forcing EBOD to borrow global pooled RRs. [ijhsr](https://www.ijhsr.org/IJHSR_Vol.9_Issue.6_June2019/36.pdf)

2. **Pathogen‑specific flood–diarrhoea effect estimates in Indian settings.**  
   Global reviews document strong flood‑related increases for specific pathogens (e.g. paratyphoid, cryptosporidiosis, cholera), but similar pathogen‑resolved RRs or ORs from Indian cohorts or outbreak datasets are scarce, limiting etiological resolution in EBOD. [journals.sagepub](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1068/a32193)

3. **Standardized measurement of flood exposure and infrastructure failure in Indian diarrhoeal studies.**  
   Many Indian outbreak reports qualitatively link flooding, damaged pipelines, and contamination but lack standardized metrics of flood intensity, duration, inundation, or WASH infrastructure performance, which complicates deriving robust dose–response functions for EBOD.
