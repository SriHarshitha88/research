## Main Methodological Synthesis

Across the reviewed literature, the dominant burden-estimation logic is a comparative risk assessment chain: exposure characterization, selection of relative risk or exposure-response function, calculation of attributable fraction, application to baseline mortality or morbidity, and conversion to YLL, YLD, and DALYs.

### Common EBOD Pipeline

1. Define climate or environmental exposure
2. Select exposure-response relationship or relative risk
3. Define counterfactual or minimum-risk exposure
4. Calculate attributable fraction or population attributable fraction
5. Apply the attributable fraction to baseline burden
6. Convert attributable burden into YLL, YLD, and DALYs
7. Quantify uncertainty and report assumptions

### Recurring Input Requirements

- Exposure distribution data
- Relative risk or exposure-response coefficients
- Baseline disease incidence, prevalence, or mortality
- Population and demographic structure
- Life tables and disability weights
- Burden inputs for YLL, YLD, and DALY estimation
- Uncertainty bounds for exposure and effect parameters

## Implications for the India Climate-Health EBOD Study

The review shows that India adaptation should combine WHO-style EBOD framing, GBD-aligned comparative risk assessment, India-specific response functions where available, and explicit uncertainty handling. Air pollution and heat emerge as the most immediately computable climate-sensitive modules because they have clearer exposure metrics, stronger Indian evidence, and existing attributable burden workflows.

## Gap Before Step 2

- Disease selection still needs to be finalized for India
- India-specific RR functions are available for some exposures but not all climate-sensitive outcomes
- Exposure thresholds and counterfactual choices need to be harmonized across disease modules
- Baseline burden inputs must be mapped carefully for each shortlisted disease
- Climate-only pathways remain less standardized than air-pollution modules

### Step 2 Expansion - Cardiovascular Disease and Source-Specific PM2.5

- Rationale: Step 2 should now include a dedicated cardiovascular evidence cluster rather than treating PM2.5 only as a generic air-pollution module.
- Exposure framing for this cluster: industry emissions, vehicle emissions, construction dust, and road dust/tar-road resuspension as source-linked PM2.5 pathways.
- Scope note: inhaler aerosols are clinically important for respiratory patients but are not a primary ambient PM2.5 source in EBOD source-apportionment workflows; keep them as a contextual modifier, not a core exposure source.

### Step 2 Review Direction - Add More Disease-Specific Climate Evidence

- Decision: Yes, include more papers in Step 2 even if methodology differs, as long as they clearly support a climate/exposure -> disease pathway.
- Goal for Step 2: strengthen disease selection and evidence grading, not finalize one uniform method.
- Rule: prioritize papers that improve exposure-outcome confidence and can guide Step 3 RR extraction.

#### Inclusion Logic for Additional Step 2 Papers

- Include if paper gives clear linkage between a climate/environmental exposure and a specific disease outcome.
- Include if paper reports RR/HR/OR or attributable burden signals, even when DALY is not directly estimated.
- Include if paper gives source-specific exposure evidence (industry, transport, construction, road dust, biomass, ozone interactions).
- Include if paper is India-specific or from settings with similar exposure profile to India.
- De-prioritize papers with weak exposure definition or no usable outcome specification.

#### Priority Disease Clusters for Additional Step 2 Search

- Cardiovascular: ischemic heart disease, stroke, hypertension-related outcomes.
- Respiratory: COPD, asthma, lower respiratory infections.
- Heat outcomes: all-cause mortality, heat stroke, cardio-respiratory mortality.
- Water and sanitation pathways: diarrhoeal disease and enteric infections.
- Vector-borne: dengue, malaria, chikungunya where climate suitability is quantified.
- Nutrition-linked: child undernutrition and climate-sensitive food insecurity pathways.

#### Practical Target for Step 2 Expansion

- Add 5-8 additional papers per high-priority disease cluster.
- Keep a balanced mix of India studies, South Asia studies, and high-quality global evidence.
- Tag each paper as `High`, `Medium`, or `Low` for Step 3 extraction readiness.
- Mark each paper as `Directly usable` (has extractable effect size fields) or `Context-only`.
