Main Methodological Synthesis

Across the review, the dominant logic is a comparative risk assessment chain that moves from exposure characterization to response-function selection, attributable fraction estimation, application to baseline burden, and conversion into YLL, YLD, and DALYs. This pipeline is highly reusable for India, especially for heat and air-pollution modules.

Common EBOD Pipeline

  1. Define climate or environmental exposure
  2. Select exposure-response relationship or relative risk
  3. Define counterfactual or minimum-risk exposure
  4. Calculate attributable fraction or population attributable fraction
  5. Apply the attributable fraction to baseline mortality or morbidity
  6. Convert attributable burden into YLL, YLD, and DALYs
  7. Quantify uncertainty and report assumptions

Recurring Input Requirements

  • Exposure distribution data
  • Relative risk or exposure-response coefficients
  • Baseline disease incidence, prevalence, or mortality
  • Population and demographic structure
  • Life tables and disability weights
  • Burden inputs for YLL, YLD, and DALY estimation
  • Uncertainty bounds for exposure and effect parameters

Top 10 Highlighted Papers

1. Introduction and methods: Environmental burden of disease at national level

Citation: World Health Organization. Introduction and methods: Environmental burden of disease at national level. WHO Environmental Burden of Disease Series, No. 1. Geneva: World Health Organization; 2004.

Framework: Environmental Burden of Disease (EBD) framework based on: Comparative Risk Assessment (CRA) Population Attributable Fraction (PAF) Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs) (Page 10-18)

Direct reuse: PAF equations DALY/YLL/YLD framework Comparative risk assessment structure Workflow sequence for burden estimation Exposure-response integration logic Uncertainty analysis structure National-level burden estimation framework Counterfactual exposure methodology

Main findings: Environmental burden estimation can be standardized across countries.

Limitations: Data limitations in low-resource settings Uncertainty in exposure-response relationships Limited exposure monitoring in many countries Dependence on assumptions for counterfactual exposure levels Incomplete epidemiological evidence for some environmental risks (Page 23-25)

Implications for the India Climate-Health EBOD Study

The review supports building the Indian climate-health EBOD study around WHO-style EBOD methods, GBD-aligned comparative risk assessment, and India-specific response functions wherever they are available. Heat and air pollution currently have the strongest evidence base because they offer clearer exposure metrics, more mature response functions, and existing attributable burden workflows.

Gap Before Step 2

  • Disease selection still needs to be finalized for India.
  • India-specific RR functions exist for some but not all climate-sensitive outcomes.
  • Counterfactual exposure choices still need harmonization across disease modules.
  • Baseline burden inputs must be mapped cleanly for each shortlisted disease.
  • Climate-only pathways remain less standardized than air-pollution modules.
